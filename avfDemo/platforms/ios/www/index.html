Research

Week 1:

1: The login scenario can be covered by requiring only one login for multiple services, similar to apps that have the option to login using your Facebook credentials. At first I didn't understand how Instagram could be used in a payment scenario. Then I thought one might be able to use Instagram as a way to take a picture of a check and have that check sent to another Instagram user. Twitter partnered with a major credit card company (can't recall which) to accomplish something similar.

2: JSONP is used as a way to avoid the same origin policy and grab data from a server other than one hosting your web page. There are some security concerns involved that I have found can be avoided by using another technique called CORS.

3: Pairing the Facebook API with the native GPS could allow a musician to see which 'friends' of their band are near the venue and could even automatically send a status update announcing the band's arrival. Another mash up could be to combine the google maps API with native storage to store directions in case of a loss of cell or Internet service.

Week 2:

1: I’m going to have to look a little closer at my code and actually write it with a bit of an eye toward accessibility. I know that various screen readers and such will pick some of the tags up. The mobile space seems to be a better area for ease of access. We’re forced to simplify our designs and only give the users what matters most. Desktop web pages are very busy and that can be overwhelming.

2: The internet in general isn’t really built to be accessible, especially for the blind. It’s no longer just a medium of text like it once was. There are so many images and visual cues that can get lost when they’re reduced to a robot voice describing it. Which is another point, the voices are horrible and grating. I’m sure there’s plenty of room for improvement there.

3: This guy built an XBOX 360 controller that is playable with only one hand. It may not apply to the mobile space, but this is one of the coolest things I’ve ever seen that aids in accessibility. http://benheck.com/Games/Xbox360/controls/1hand/singlehandcontroller.htm During my poking around I came across something that really blew my mind, Braille Displays! These are devices that pair with screen reader software and occasionally speech synthesizers to enable blind users to read text output. They even make some of these displays for mobile devices.

4: The first app I found was called TalkBack for android. And it seems Apple has taken the accessibility thing to heart with its integration of VoiceOver which is available for both OS X and iOS.
